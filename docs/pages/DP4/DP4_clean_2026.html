DP4: GenAI Literacy

Author

Dr. Nikolas Johnston

Published

May 1, 2026

Welcome to Dry Practical 4

NoteLearning Objectives

By the end of this session, you will be able to:

  1. Distinguish between AI use that supports your learning and AI use that replaces it
  2. Construct effective prompts using four prompt types (Context, Do-for-me, Evaluate, Teach-me) tailored to scientific writing
  3. Revise your own scientific writing using AI as a tool while retaining your voice and accountability
  4. Reflect critically on the differences between human peer feedback and AI feedback
ImportantWhy does this matter?

Generative AI tools like Microsoft Copilot, ChatGPT, and Google Gemini are now part of the scientific landscape. Researchers use them to refine drafts, check logic, and brainstorm — but they also produce confident-sounding text that is sometimes factually wrong, structurally misleading, or scientifically shallow.

Meanwhile, research shows students often struggle to identify where acceptable AI use ends and misconduct begins. Chan (2024) surveyed 393 university students and found a clear gradient: students readily identified direct AI copy-paste as misconduct, but when scenarios involved editing, verifying, and rewriting AI output, agreement dropped sharply. This practical is designed to help you navigate that uncertain middle ground — developing your understanding of the ethical use of AI so you can make those judgements with confidence.

NoteBefore you begin

Have the following ready:

  1. Your draft scientific report (ideally with peer review comments from DP3)
  2. A new Microsoft Word document to record your answers
  3. Microsoft Copilot or another AI tool of your choice open in a browser tab (link below)
  4. Tracked changes enabled in Word or Google Docs

Part 1: The authorship line — when does AI use become misconduct?

Before you touch a prompt, you need a framework for thinking about when AI use is appropriate and when it crosses a line. This is not a simple binary — it is a spectrum, and where you fall on it depends on whether you maintained intellectual control over the work.

The Authorship Spectrum

Chan (2024) developed a 12-point AI-giarism scale that maps AI use scenarios from fully original work down to direct copy-paste. Her data from 393 students revealed behaviours at the extremes were easy to judge, but scenarios in the middle grey zone were much more uncertain.

Below we have prepared a spectrum that groups these scenarios into three zones. As you work through the tasks that follow, you are practising your evaluative judgement of the type of use that is often the most difficult to classify as ethical or not.

✓ AI supports your thinking — YOU ARE THE AUTHOR Chan's F8–F12 · Students generally agree not misconduct
F12. You completed the assessment without the use of any AI tools.
F10–F11. You use AI to check grammar, or as a search engine for relevant sources— no AI generated text appears in your submission.
F9. You write your own draft, seek feedback from AI, and make improvements based on its suggestions. You acknowledged AI use.
F8. You use AI to rephrase some of your own written content to improve writing quality. You acknowledged AI use.
⚠ Grey zone — support or substitution? — CAUTION Chan's F3–F7 · Students are uncertain if misconduct
F7. AI generates initial ideas, you supplement with your own. After verifying facts, you rewrote most sections and submitted the work as your own. You acknowledged AI use.
F6. AI generates initial ideas, you supplement with your own. After verifying facts, you submitted work with parts generated by AI and parts written by you. You acknowledged AI use.
F4–F5. You began with your own ideas, used AI to produce multiple responses, verified facts, then selected the best parts, made edits, and submitted. You acknowledged AI use.
F3. You prompted AI, verified all facts, made edits, added references, formatted the response, and submitted it as your own. You acknowledged AI use.
The question: did you do the thinking, or just the editing?
✗ AI substitutes for your thinking — YOU ARE NOT THE AUTHOR Chan's F1–F2 · Students agree: this is misconduct
F1. You prompted AI, copied the generated response, and submitted it as your own work. You acknowledged AI use.
F2. You used AI to paraphrase texts from other sources without acknowledging those sources. You acknowledged AI use.

Note: In both cases the student acknowledged using AI — but acknowledgement alone does not make it acceptable. The intellectual work was outsourced, and the student cannot take accountability for content they did not create or verify.

ImportantThe accountability test

You can apply a simple test to any sentence in your report:

Could you explain and defend this sentence if asked — including why you chose these words, what evidence supports this claim, and where the data came from?

If the answer is no — if there are sentences you do not fully understand, claims you have not verified, or references you have not read — then you have lost authorship, regardless of how much of the text you technically typed.

Task: Where Does Each Scenario Fall?

Read each scenario and decide where it falls on the authorship spectrum. Check your answer, then discuss with your neighbour.

Scenario 1

A student asks Copilot: “Rewrite my discussion to sound more academic.” The AI produces a polished version. The student reads it, thinks “this sounds much better than mine,” and pastes it into their report with no changes.

Scenario 2

A student asks Copilot: “What are the key features of a strong discussion section in a biology report?” The AI explains that discussions should link results to hypotheses, compare findings with published literature, and acknowledge limitations. The student uses these principles to restructure their own discussion, writing every sentence themselves.

Scenario 3

A student asks Copilot to “add relevant references” to their discussion. The AI inserts three citations, including “Zhang et al. (2019)” which claims to show mitochondrial dysfunction in a related cell line. The student includes the citation without searching for the paper. The paper does not exist.

Scenario 4

A student pastes their methods section into Copilot and asks: “Is there enough detail here for someone to replicate my experiment?” The AI responds that the centrifugation speed and duration are missing. The student adds those details in their own words.

Scenario 5

A student asks Copilot to rewrite their results paragraph. The AI version is more concise and better structured. The student compares both versions carefully, takes two phrases from the AI version, rewrites the rest themselves, and checks that all data values match their original spreadsheet.

TipDiscuss with your neighbour

Were there any scenarios where you disagreed? What made the difference between “author” and “grey zone”? What is the minimum amount of critical evaluation needed before you can claim authorship of a revised sentence?

Part 2: Catching AI errors

AI tools are fluent but not reliable. They produce text that sounds correct even when it is wrong. This is not a theoretical concern — it is empirically documented at alarming rates.Even elite peer review fails to catch these errors. If expert scientists miss these errors, you should assume you will too — unless you verify every AI-generated claim.

Task: Spot the Problem

Each example below shows AI-generated text produced in response to a student’s prompt. Identify what went wrong.

Example 1:

Student prompt: “Add a reference to support my claim that citrate synthase is a mitochondrial matrix enzyme.”

AI output: “Citrate synthase is a well-established marker of the mitochondrial matrix (Hargreaves & Bhatt, 2020). Its activity is commonly used to assess mitochondrial content and integrity in both clinical and research settings.”

Example 2:

Student’s original: “Citrate synthase activity was lower in the diseased cell line compared to the healthy control, which may indicate impaired mitochondrial function in this model.”

AI rewrite: “Citrate synthase activity was significantly reduced in diseased cells, confirming mitochondrial dysfunction as a hallmark of this disease.”

Example 3:

Student prompt: “Make my results section more detailed. Citrate synthase specific activity in the mitochondrial fraction was increased in healthy cells and decreased in diseased cells (p = 0.003, Student’s t-test).”

AI output: “Citrate synthase specific activity in the mitochondrial fraction was 45.2 ± 2.8 nmol/min/mg in healthy cells and 31.6 ± 3.4 nmol/min/mg in diseased cells (p = 0.003, Student’s t-test).”

WarningThe verification checklist — accountability in practice

Every time you use AI-generated text in your report, apply the accountability test. Can you defend each of these?

  1. References — Search for every citation the AI provides in PubMed or Google Scholar. Does the paper exist? Does it say what the AI claims?
  2. Data values — Cross-check every number against your original data.
  3. Strength of claims — Has the AI upgraded “may suggest” to “confirms” or “demonstrates”? Your undergraduate experiment with limited replication almost certainly does not support definitive claims.
  4. Added content — Has the AI introduced information, mechanisms, or comparisons that were not in your original text? If you cannot verify them against a source you have read, remove them.
  5. Removed qualifiers — Has the AI stripped out important caveats like “in this experiment” or “under these conditions”?

Part 3: Prompt engineering for scientific writing and hands on report revision

Not all prompts are equal. The way you talk to an AI tool determines the quality of what you get back. In this section you will learn four prompt types, practise each one on your own report, and critically evaluate the results.

The Four Prompt Types

🧠 Context Prompt

Purpose: Set the AI's role so its output matches your discipline and audience.

Example: "You are a biology lecturer who marks third-year scientific reports on enzyme biochemistry. The reports follow a standard report structure."

Why it works: Without context, the AI defaults to generic advice. With it, feedback can be calibrated to your specific task and standard.

🔧 "Do for me" Prompt

Purpose: Delegate a specific writing task to the AI.

Example: "Rewrite the following results paragraph so that it reports only what the data show, without interpretation. Keep all numerical values and statistical tests intact."

Why it works: Specific constraints produce better output. Caution: You must critically evaluate the result — the AI may alter data, change meaning, or introduce errors. If you cannot articulate why the AI's version is better than yours, you have not learned from the process.

🔍 "Evaluate" Prompt

Purpose: Ask the AI to critique your writing rather than rewrite it.

Example: "Read my discussion section. Does my interpretation of the citrate synthase data logically follow from the results I reported? Identify any logical gaps or unsupported claims."

Why it works: This supports metacognition — you remain in control of the writing, but gain an external perspective on its logic and structure.

🎓 "Teach me" Prompt

Purpose: Deepen your understanding of a writing principle or scientific concept.

Example: "Explain the difference between reporting results and interpreting results in a scientific report. Give me an example using citrate synthase activity data."

Why it works: This builds your skill rather than outsourcing it. The AI teaches you a principle you then apply yourself.

You will now apply each prompt type to your own scientific report. For each step, record your prompt, the AI output, your evaluation, and what you changed. This forms the basis of your Assessment 5 Pro Forma.

ImportantCritical rule

Do not paste your entire report into the AI. Work with one paragraph or section at a time. This gives you more control, makes evaluation easier, and reduces the risk of the AI fabricating connections between sections.

Step 1: Set the Context

Write a context prompt that tells the AI who it is and what it is reviewing. Use this as your opening message before any other prompts.

Your context prompt:

Write a prompt that specifies:

  • The AI’s role (e.g., biology lecturer, journal reviewer, scientific editor)
  • The type of document (third-year scientific report)
  • The subject matter (citrate synthase activity in subcellular fractions)
  • The assessment criteria (clarity, accuracy, evidence-based reasoning, IMRaD structure)

Record your prompt in your Word document.

Step 2: “Do for me” — Targeted Revision

Choose a paragraph from your report that you know needs improvement. Paste it into Copilot with a specific “do for me” prompt.

Before you accept anything the AI returns, check:

Record: your prompt, the AI output, what you changed, and what you rejected.

Step 3: “Evaluate” — Getting a Critique

Choose a different section (ideally your discussion) and ask the AI to evaluate it rather than rewrite it.

Good evaluate prompts for biology reports:

  • “Does my interpretation of [specific result] logically follow from the data I presented in my results section?”
  • “Are there any claims in this paragraph that are not supported by the data or references I cite?”
  • “Does this discussion section address the original hypothesis stated in my introduction?”

Record: your prompt, the AI’s critique, and what action you took in response.

Step 4: “Teach me” — Building Your Understanding

Ask the AI to explain a writing or science concept you found difficult during this report.

Example teach-me prompts:

  • “What is the difference between a result and an interpretation in a biology report? Give me an example using enzyme activity data.”
  • “How should I structure a discussion paragraph that compares my findings to published literature?”
  • “Why is it important to normalise enzyme activity to protein content, and how should I explain this in my methods?”

Record: your prompt, the key insight you gained, and how it changed your approach.
