DP3: Peer Review – Learning to Evaluate and Improve Scientific Writing

Welcome to Dry Practical 3

NoteLearning Objectives

By the end of this session, you will be able to:

  1. Calibrate your understanding of the marking rubric by evaluating sample abstracts and report sections
  2. Distinguish between ineffective and constructive peer feedback
  3. Write specific, actionable review comments using a structured feedback framework
  4. Use Generative AI to assist in revising writing in response to peer feedback
  5. Complete formal peer reviews of your classmates’ scientific reports in FeedbackFruits
ImportantWhy does peer review matter?

Peer review is not just a university exercise — it is the cornerstone of how science works. Every published scientific paper has been scrutinised by other scientists before it appears in a journal. Learning to critically evaluate others’ writing, and to receive and respond to feedback on your own, are core professional skills in science and beyond.

When you review a peer’s work, you are practising the same skill set used by working scientists, journal editors, and grant assessors. Good peer review benefits both the reviewer and the author: reviewers develop sharper critical reading skills, and authors receive feedback they can act on to improve their work.

Today’s session prepares you for Assessment 4, where you will revise your own scientific report using both human peer feedback and Generative AI tools, and reflect on that process.

⏱ Estimated session time: 2 hours | Parts 1–4 are training activities (~70 min) | Part 5 is live peer review (~50 min)

Part 1: Rubric Calibration

Before you can give useful feedback, you need to internalise the standards. In this section, you will read sample abstracts describing the same experiment and match each to a rubric level.

TipThe Experiment

All abstracts below describe the same study: measuring citrate synthase activity in subcellular fractions (mitochondrial and cytosolic) of healthy and diseased mammalian cell lines. This mirrors the work you completed in Wet Labs 1–3.

The Rubric: Abstract Criteria

Refer to the abstract section of your full marking rubric. In summary, an abstract should include:

Component What to look for
Purpose 1–2 lines stating the question or aim
Experimental design 1–2 lines summarising the approach
Major results Brief description of key findings or trends
Interpretation 1–2 lines of conclusions drawn from the data
Scientific style Appropriate voice, tense, precision, and conciseness

Task: Rate Each Abstract

Read each abstract carefully. Use the rubric to assign a level, then check your answer. Write brief notes in your notebook explaining your reasoning — you will need this skill shortly.

Abstract A

Citrate synthase activity was investigated in healthy and diseased mammalian cell lines by measuring enzymatic function in cytosolic and mitochondrial fractions. Cells were lysed and separated using differential centrifugation, and activity was measured using a spectrophotometric assay. The mitochondrial fractions consistently showed higher activity, confirming the expected localization. Diseased cells exhibited lower activity overall, especially in the mitochondrial fraction. These results suggest mitochondrial function is compromised in the diseased condition. The experiment provides insight into cellular metabolic changes associated with disease.

Abstract B

We tested citrate synthase activity in different parts of the cell. Healthy and diseased cells were used in the study. We broke the cells up and spun them to get different parts, like mitochondria and cytosol. Then we measured enzyme activity with a color test. There were some differences between the samples. It looks like mitochondria might be involved in this, but it’s not totally clear from the results.

Abstract C

The purpose of this experiment was to analyze the specific activity of citrate synthase in subcellular fractions of two mammalian cell lines. Healthy and diseased cells were lysed and processed to separate mitochondria and cytosol using centrifugation. Enzyme activity was measured using a colorimetric assay and normalized to total protein content. Mitochondrial fractions had greater activity, and diseased cells showed lower activity in both compartments. These findings support the hypothesis that mitochondrial dysfunction contributes to disease. Clear trends were observed, although further validation and controls would strengthen the results.

Abstract D

This experiment aimed to investigate citrate synthase activity in cell fractions from healthy and diseased cells. Cells were prepared and divided into cytosolic and mitochondrial fractions using standard lab procedures. Enzyme activity was assessed with a colorimetric method, and we compared levels across the fractions and cell types. While mitochondrial activity appeared higher, details about the data or significance were limited. The abstract touches on all sections but lacks depth in interpreting results and establishing relevance.

Tricky Abstract: When Complexity Masks Weakness

This abstract may sound advanced but contains significant flaws. Your task is to see past the jargon.

Abstract E

Citrate synthase SA was quantified in MT and CYT subfractions isolated from immortalized mammalian CLs of divergent physiological states (i.e., HL and DL). Fractionation was executed via DC and analyzed through TNB-based spectrophotometric methodologies. Comparative SA metrics were demonstrated to be variably reduced across conditions, though significance thresholds were not consistently upheld. Interpretation was limited by inconsistent normalization to TP content and variability in the enzymatic assay kinetics. It is inferred that mitochondrial biogenesis or bioenergetic perturbation may be implicated, although further ELISA and qPCR corroboration would be recommended.

WarningWhy this abstract is ‘Fair’ despite sounding complex
Problem Explanation
Unexplained acronyms SA, MT, CYT, CLs, HL/DL, DC, TP — a reader shouldn’t need a decoder ring
Passive voice throughout Obscures who did what; reduces clarity and accountability
Tense inconsistency Shifts from past (“was quantified”) to present (“is inferred”)
Superficial complexity Complex phrasing disguises weak interpretation of results
Vague conclusions “May be implicated” and “would be recommended” are hedged to the point of saying nothing

Key lesson: Good scientific writing is clear, not complex. If a reader has to re-read a sentence three times, the writing has failed — no matter how many technical terms it contains.

TipDiscuss with your neighbour

Compare your scores across all five abstracts. Where did you agree? Where did you disagree? What rubric criteria helped you resolve disagreements?

Part 2: Learning to Give Feedback

You can now evaluate writing — but can you help someone improve it? Giving constructive feedback is a different skill. In this section, you will learn to distinguish useful feedback from unhelpful feedback, and practise writing your own.

What Makes Feedback Useful?

NoteThe DES Framework: Describe → Evaluate → Suggest

Every piece of feedback you write should follow three steps:

D Describe — Point to the specific part of the text you are commenting on. Quote or paraphrase it so the author knows exactly what you mean.

E Evaluate — Explain why this is a strength or a problem, using the rubric language where possible. Don’t just say “this is wrong” — say what standard it falls short of.

S Suggest — Offer a concrete, actionable revision or direction. The author should be able to read your comment and know what to do next.

Task: Rate These Reviewer Comments

Below are six example reviewer comments responding to Abstract A (the “Excellent” abstract from Part 1). Rate each comment as Unhelpful, Adequate, or Excellent feedback.

Comment 1

“Looks good overall.”

Comment 2

“The abstract doesn’t mention specific activity or protein normalisation. Since the rubric asks for enough information to understand the experiments, adding that enzyme activity was normalised to protein content would strengthen the methods description and move this toward Outstanding.”

Comment 3

“This is wrong. You need to fix the abstract.”

Comment 4

“The last sentence (‘The experiment provides insight into cellular metabolic changes associated with disease’) is quite vague. Consider specifying what kind of metabolic change — e.g., reduced mitochondrial enzyme activity — to make the conclusion more informative.”

Comment 5

“The abstract covers the main points but could be improved.”

Comment 6

“Good clear structure. The aim, method, results and conclusion are all present, which matches the rubric criteria for a strong abstract. One strength is that you describe the direction of results (diseased cells had lower activity), not just that differences existed.”

TipKey Patterns

Notice what the excellent comments have in common:

  • They point to specific text (not generalities)
  • They explain why something is strong or weak (often referencing the rubric)
  • They suggest a concrete action the author could take
  • Positive feedback is just as specific as critical feedback — “good structure” is vague; explaining which structural elements work and why is useful

What the unhelpful comments have in common:

  • They are vague (“looks good”, “needs work”)
  • They offer no direction for improvement
  • They may be dismissive or discouraging without being constructive

Practice: Write Your Own Feedback

Your task: Go back to Abstract D (the “Fair” abstract from Part 1). Using the DES framework, write two feedback comments in your notebook or a text document:

  1. One comment on a strength — identify something the abstract does reasonably well. Be specific about what and why.
  2. One comment on an area for improvement — identify a specific weakness, explain why it matters (connect to the rubric), and suggest how the author could fix it.

Time: 5 minutes

When you’re done, share your comments with the person next to you. Discuss: Are your comments specific enough that the author would know exactly what to change?

Part 3: Beyond the Abstract — Practising on Results

Your peer reviews will cover entire reports, not just abstracts. This section extends your calibration to results sections and data presentation — areas where students often find it harder to give feedback.

Results Text: Describing vs. Discussing

A common mistake in results sections is discussing the results instead of describing them. The rubric explicitly penalises this. Read the two versions below and decide which one correctly handles the boundary between Results and Discussion.

Version X

Citrate synthase specific activity was higher in the mitochondrial fraction compared to the cytosolic fraction in both cell lines (Figure 1). The healthy cell line showed a mean mitochondrial specific activity of 42.3 ± 3.1 nmol/min/mg, while the diseased cell line showed 28.7 ± 4.2 nmol/min/mg. This difference was statistically significant (p < 0.05). The lower activity in diseased cells likely reflects impaired mitochondrial biogenesis, consistent with previous reports of metabolic dysfunction in this disease model (Smith et al., 2021).

Version Y

Citrate synthase specific activity was higher in the mitochondrial fraction compared to the cytosolic fraction in both cell lines (Figure 1). The healthy cell line showed a mean mitochondrial specific activity of 42.3 ± 3.1 nmol/min/mg, while the diseased cell line showed 28.7 ± 4.2 nmol/min/mg. This difference was statistically significant (p < 0.05). These data indicate that enzyme activity was concentrated in the mitochondrial compartment, and that the diseased cell line had reduced mitochondrial citrate synthase activity relative to the healthy control.

Quick Reference: DES Framework Sentence Starters

If you’re stuck on how to phrase feedback, these sentence starters can help:

Describe
"In the section where you write…"
"Figure X shows…"
"Your discussion of [topic]…"
"The caption for Figure X states…"
Evaluate
"This works well because…"
"According to the rubric, this would benefit from…"
"This is strong because it clearly shows…"
"The issue here is that the reader cannot…"
Suggest
"Consider revising this to include…"
"One way to improve this would be…"
"Adding [specific detail] would strengthen…"
"Try reorganising so that…"
