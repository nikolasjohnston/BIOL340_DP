DP3: Peer Review – Learning to Evaluate and Improve Scientific Writing

Welcome to Dry Practical 3

NoteLearning Objectives

By the end of this session, you will be able to:

  1. Calibrate your understanding of the marking rubric by evaluating sample abstracts and report sections
  2. Identify the features of peer feedback that research shows lead to actual revisions
  3. Write specific, actionable review comments using a structured feedback framework
  4. Recognise why hedging language in feedback reduces its effectiveness
  5. Use Generative AI to assist in revising writing in response to peer feedback
  6. Complete formal peer reviews of your classmates’ scientific reports in FeedbackFruits
ImportantWhy does peer review matter?

Peer review is not just a university exercise — it is the cornerstone of how science works. Every published scientific paper has been scrutinised by other scientists before it appears in a journal. Learning to critically evaluate others’ writing, and to receive and respond to feedback on your own, are core professional skills in science and beyond.

When you review a peer’s work, you are practising the same skill set used by working scientists, journal editors, and grant assessors. Good peer review benefits both the reviewer and the author: reviewers develop sharper critical reading skills, and authors receive feedback they can act on to improve their work.

Today’s session prepares you for Assessment 4, where you will revise your own scientific report using both human peer feedback and Generative AI tools, and reflect on that process.

Part 1: Rubric Calibration

Before you can give useful feedback, you need to get used to using the rubric (i.e. internalise the standards). In this section, you will read sample abstracts describing the same experiment and match each to a rubric level.

TipThe Experiment

All abstracts below describe the same study: measuring citrate synthase activity in subcellular fractions (mitochondrial and cytosolic) of healthy and diseased mammalian cell lines. This mirrors the work you completed in the Wet Labs.

The Rubric: Abstract Criteria

Refer to the abstract section of your full marking rubric. In summary, an abstract should include:

Component What to look for
Purpose 1–2 lines stating the question or aim
Experimental design 1–2 lines summarising the approach
Major results Brief description of key findings or trends
Interpretation 1–2 lines of conclusions drawn from the data
Scientific style Appropriate voice, tense, precision, and conciseness

Task: Rate Each Abstract

Read each abstract carefully. Use the rubric to assign a level, then check your answer. Write brief notes in your notebook explaining your reasoning — you will need this skill shortly.

Abstract A

Citrate synthase activity was investigated in healthy and diseased mammalian cell lines by measuring enzymatic function in cytosolic and mitochondrial fractions. Cells were lysed and separated using differential centrifugation, and activity was measured using a spectrophotometric assay. The mitochondrial fractions consistently showed higher activity, confirming the expected localization. Diseased cells exhibited lower activity overall, especially in the mitochondrial fraction. These results suggest mitochondrial function is compromised in the diseased condition. The experiment provides insight into cellular metabolic changes associated with disease.

Abstract B

We tested citrate synthase activity in different parts of the cell. Healthy and diseased cells were used in the study. We broke the cells up and spun them to get different parts, like mitochondria and cytosol. Then we measured enzyme activity with a color test. There were some differences between the samples. It looks like mitochondria might be involved in this, but it’s not totally clear from the results.

Abstract C

The purpose of this experiment was to analyze the specific activity of citrate synthase in subcellular fractions of two mammalian cell lines. Healthy and diseased cells were lysed and processed to separate mitochondria and cytosol using centrifugation. Enzyme activity was measured using a colorimetric assay and normalized to total protein content. Mitochondrial fractions had greater activity, and diseased cells showed lower activity in both compartments. These findings support the hypothesis that mitochondrial dysfunction contributes to disease. Clear trends were observed, although further validation and controls would strengthen the results.

Abstract D

This experiment aimed to investigate citrate synthase activity in cell fractions from healthy and diseased cells. Cells were prepared and divided into cytosolic and mitochondrial fractions using standard lab procedures. Enzyme activity was assessed with a colorimetric method, and we compared levels across the fractions and cell types. While mitochondrial activity appeared higher, details about the data or significance were limited. The abstract touches on all sections but lacks depth in interpreting results and establishing relevance.

Tricky Abstract: When Complexity Masks Weakness

This abstract may sound advanced but contains significant flaws. Your task is to see past the jargon.

Abstract E

Citrate synthase SA was quantified in MT and CYT subfractions isolated from immortalized mammalian CLs of divergent physiological states (i.e., HL and DL). Fractionation was executed via DC and analyzed through TNB-based spectrophotometric methodologies. Comparative SA metrics were demonstrated to be variably reduced across conditions, though significance thresholds were not consistently upheld. Interpretation was limited by inconsistent normalization to TP content and variability in the enzymatic assay kinetics. It is inferred that mitochondrial biogenesis or bioenergetic perturbation may be implicated, although further ELISA and qPCR corroboration would be recommended.

WarningWhy this abstract is ‘Fair’ despite sounding complex
Problem Explanation
Unexplained acronyms SA, MT, CYT, CLs, HL/DL, DC, TP — a reader shouldn’t need a decoder ring
Passive voice throughout Obscures who did what; reduces clarity and accountability
Tense inconsistency Shifts from past (“was quantified”) to present (“is inferred”)
Superficial complexity Complex phrasing disguises weak interpretation of results
Vague conclusions “May be implicated” and “would be recommended” are hedged to the point of saying nothing

Key lesson: Good scientific writing is clear, not complex. If a reader has to re-read a sentence three times, the writing has failed — no matter how many technical terms it contains.

TipDiscuss with your neighbour

Compare your scores across all five abstracts. Where did you agree? Where did you disagree? What rubric criteria helped you resolve disagreements?

Part 2: What Type of Feedback Actually Leads to Revision?

You can now evaluate writing — but can you help someone improve it? Giving constructive feedback is a different skill. Research tells us a lot about what makes the difference between feedback that gets ignored and feedback that actually leads to better writing.

The Research: Feedback Features That Matter

The framework we use in this activity is adapted from Wu & Schunn (2020), who studied what features of peer feedback predict whether authors actually revise their work. They analysed over 3,600 peer feedback comments and tracked which ones led to actual revisions. They identified four cognitive features and two affective features that shape how feedback is received:

Cognitive Features

Identification — Explicitly stating what the problem is and where it occurs in the text

Explanation — Stating why something is a problem (or a strength)

Suggestion — Providing general advice on how to improve (e.g., "add more evidence")

Solution — Providing a specific, concrete fix (e.g., "add a sentence stating the p-value from your t-test")

Affective Features

Mitigating praise — Positive feedback attached directly to a criticism, which softens the blow and increases agreement with the critique

Hedges — Words like "maybe", "perhaps", "I think", "possibly", "a little" — intended to be polite but shown to reduce the likelihood that feedback is acted on

NoteWhat the research found

Two findings from Wu & Schunn (2020) are especially important for your peer reviews today:

  1. Understanding the problem and feedback was the strongest predictor of revision. When a reviewer explained why something was a problem, provided information on what they think could be a solution the author was more likely to revise — even if they didn’t explicitly agree with the feedback. In contrast, if an author doesn’t understand the feedback they almost certainly will not implement it.

  2. Hedges reduced revision rates. When reviewers softened their constructive advice with hedging language (“maybe you could…”, “perhaps consider…”), authors were significantly less likely to act on it. This effect was not because hedges reduced understanding or agreement — rather, hedges appear to undermine the implicit persuasive force of a comment, possibly by signalling that the reviewer lacks confidence or expertise.

The bottom line: be specific, provide detail, and say it with confidence.

Reference: Wu, Y. & Schunn, C.D. (2020). From feedback to revisions: Effects of feedback features and perceptions. Contemporary Educational Psychology, 60, 101826.

The DES Framework

Based on these research findings, every piece of feedback you write should include three components:

D Describe — Point to the specific part of the text you are commenting on. Quote or paraphrase it so the author knows exactly what you mean. (This is Wu & Schunn’s “identification” feature.)

E Explain — State why this is a strength or a problem, connecting it to the rubric criteria where possible. Don’t just say “this is wrong” — say what standard it falls short of and why it matters for the reader. (This is Wu & Schunn’s “explanation” feature — the strongest predictor of revision.)

S Suggest or Solve — Offer a concrete, actionable direction for improvement. The more specific you can be, the better — a specific solution (“add the p-value from your t-test”) is more effective than a vague suggestion (“add more stats”). (Wu & Schunn found that specific solutions were better understood than general suggestions.)

The Problem with Hedges

It is natural to want to be polite when giving criticism. You might instinctively reach for softening language: “Maybe you could…”, “I think perhaps this might…”, “This is possibly a little unclear…”

The research shows this instinct, while kind, backfires. Wu & Schunn (2020) found that hedged feedback was significantly less likely to lead to revision. Hedges signal to the author that the reviewer is not confident in their own assessment, which gives the author an easy reason to dismiss the comment. .

Here is what hedged vs. direct feedback looks like in practice:

Hedged (less likely to be acted on)

"I think maybe you could possibly add a little more detail about the enzyme assay."
Direct (more likely to be acted on)

"The methods description needs more detail about the enzyme assay — specifically, state that activity was measured via TNB absorbance at 412 nm and normalised to total protein."
Hedged

"The conclusion seems like it might be a bit vague."
Direct

"The conclusion is vague — it says 'provides insight into metabolic changes' without specifying what those changes are. State that citrate synthase activity was reduced in the diseased mitochondrial fraction."
Hedged

"Perhaps you might want to think about whether the figure caption has enough information."
Direct

"The figure caption ('Graph of results') does not tell the reader what is being measured. According to the rubric, captions should be 'informative, concise and complete' — add the variable measured, units, cell lines, and statistical test used."
ImportantBeing direct is not being rude

Notice that the direct versions above are not harsh or unkind. They are specific, evidence-based, and constructive. The goal is to be clear and confident, not blunt or dismissive.

If you want to soften your criticism, use mitigating praise rather than hedges. For example:

“Your abstract covers all the required components clearly, however the concluding sentence is too vague. Specify what metabolic change was observed rather than writing ‘provides insight into metabolic changes’.”

This acknowledges the author’s effort while still delivering clear, actionable feedback. It avoids hedging words that undermine your credibility as a reviewer.

Task: Rate These Reviewer Comments

Below are eight example reviewer comments responding to Abstract A (the “Excellent” abstract from Part 1). Rate each comment as Unhelpful, Adequate, or Excellent feedback. Pay attention to the presence of hedges.

Comment 1

“Looks good overall.”

Comment 2

“The abstract doesn’t mention specific activity or protein normalisation. Since the rubric asks for enough information to understand the experiments, adding that enzyme activity was normalised to protein content would strengthen the methods description and move this toward Outstanding.”

Comment 3

“This is wrong. You need to fix the abstract.”

Comment 4

“The last sentence (‘The experiment provides insight into cellular metabolic changes associated with disease’) is quite vague. Consider specifying what kind of metabolic change — e.g., reduced mitochondrial enzyme activity — to make the conclusion more informative.”

Comment 5

“The abstract covers the main points but could be improved.”

Comment 6

“Good clear structure. The aim, method, results and conclusion are all present, which matches the rubric criteria for a strong abstract. One strength is that you describe the direction of results (diseased cells had lower activity), not just that differences existed.”

Comment 7

“I think maybe the last sentence could possibly be a little bit more specific? Perhaps you might want to think about adding what type of metabolic change you mean.”

Comment 8

“Your abstract clearly states the aim and method, which is a real strength. However, the final sentence — ‘The experiment provides insight into cellular metabolic changes associated with disease’ — doesn’t tell the reader what the metabolic change was. Replace this with a sentence that states the specific finding, such as: ‘Citrate synthase activity was reduced in the mitochondrial fraction of diseased cells, suggesting impaired mitochondrial metabolic function.’”

TipKey Patterns — connect these to the research

Excellent comments contain features which will encourage the author to complete their revision:

  • They identify a specific part of the text (not generalities)
  • They explain why something is strong or weak (often referencing the rubric)
  • They suggest or solve — offering a concrete direction for improvement
  • When they soften criticism, they use mitigating praise (Comment 8), not hedges

Compare Comments 4 and 7. Both address the same problem (vague concluding sentence), but Comment 4 directly identifies the issue and provides a concrete alternative, while Comment 7 buries the same point under six hedging phrases (“I think maybe… could possibly… a little bit… Perhaps you might want to think about…”). Based on Wu & Schunn’s findings, Comment 7 is significantly less likely to lead to an actual revision.

Compare Comments 7 and 8. Both try to be polite, but they use different strategies. Comment 7 uses hedges to soften; Comment 8 uses mitigating praise (“Your abstract clearly states the aim and method, which is a real strength. However…”). The research shows that mitigating praise increases agreement, while hedges decrease implementation. Comment 8 is both kinder and more effective.

Unhelpful comments lack all cognitive features — no identification, no explanation, no direction.

Part 3: Beyond the Abstract — Practising on Results and Figures

Your peer reviews will cover entire reports, not just abstracts. This section extends your calibration to results sections and data presentation — areas where students often find it harder to give feedback.

Results Text: Describing vs. Discussing

A common mistake in results sections is discussing the results instead of describing them. The rubric explicitly penalises this. Read the two versions below and decide which one correctly handles the boundary between Results and Discussion.

Version X

Citrate synthase specific activity was higher in the mitochondrial fraction compared to the cytosolic fraction in both cell lines (Figure 1). The healthy cell line showed a mean mitochondrial specific activity of 42.3 ± 3.1 nmol/min/mg, while the diseased cell line showed 28.7 ± 4.2 nmol/min/mg. This difference was statistically significant (p < 0.05). The lower activity in diseased cells likely reflects impaired mitochondrial biogenesis, consistent with previous reports of metabolic dysfunction in this disease model (Smith et al., 2021).

Version Y

Citrate synthase specific activity was higher in the mitochondrial fraction compared to the cytosolic fraction in both cell lines (Figure 1). The healthy cell line showed a mean mitochondrial specific activity of 42.3 ± 3.1 nmol/min/mg, while the diseased cell line showed 28.7 ± 4.2 nmol/min/mg. This difference was statistically significant (p < 0.05). These data indicate that enzyme activity was concentrated in the mitochondrial compartment, and that the diseased cell line had reduced mitochondrial citrate synthase activity relative to the healthy control.

Quick Reference: DES Sentence Starters

If you are stuck on how to phrase feedback, these sentence starters can help:

D — Describe / Identify
"In the section where you write..."
"Figure X shows..."
"Your discussion of [topic]..."
"The caption for Figure X states..."
E — Explain
"This works well because..."
"The rubric requires... but this section..."
"This is strong because it clearly shows..."
"The issue here is that the reader cannot..."
S — Suggest / Solve
"Revise this to include..."
"One way to improve this would be..."
"Adding [specific detail] would strengthen..."
"Reorganise so that..."

As you review, keep these questions in mind:

  • Am I being specific enough that the author knows exactly what I mean? (Describe)
  • Am I connecting my feedback to the rubric criteria? (Explanation)
  • Would the author know what to do differently after reading my comment? (Suggestion/Solution)
  • Have I acknowledged strengths with specific praise, not empty praise?
  • Have I removed hedging language that might weaken my feedback?
  • Is my score consistent with what I have written?
